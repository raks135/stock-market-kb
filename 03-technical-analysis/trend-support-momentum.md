## TL;DR
- Technical analysis (TA) infers probable future price paths from past price/volume and market-participant psychology; it rests on three Dow-derived tenets — the market discounts information, prices move in **trends**, and history tends to **repeat** (S89).
- Trend-following (moving-average crossovers, Dow theory, time-series momentum) and **cross-sectional momentum** are the best academically validated TA tools. But simple moving-average rules that "worked" historically have largely **failed out-of-sample since the late 1980s** (Fang 2014; Park & Irwin 2007) (S89, S90, S93).
- Use TA as one input inside a "fusion" framework (with fundamentals/quant), always **net transaction costs**, and guard against data-mining, look-ahead, and survivorship bias (see KB 05-stats-and-ml, 08-backtesting-methodology, 15-pitfalls-and-antipatterns).

## Core explanation
**What TA is.** Technical analysis is "the study of data generated by the action of markets and by the behavior and psychology of market participants and observers," usually applied to forecasting by interpreting past data in the context of precedent (S89). It complements — rather than replaces — fundamental analysis (which studies financial statements) and quantitative analysis (which uses statistics); all three seek to predict returns but via different means (S89).

**Three tenets (Dow-derived).** (1) Prices discount all known information; (2) prices move in identifiable trends rather than randomly; (3) history tends to repeat because market psychology is stable (S89). The third tenet is an *empirical claim*, not a law, and is exactly what efficient-market critics dispute.

**Trend.** The premise that "the trend is your friend": once a direction is established (higher highs / higher lows, or lower lows / lower highs), it is more likely to continue than to reverse (S89, Dow theory). Trend tools include moving averages (automated trend lines) and Dow-theory confirmation (S89).

**Support and resistance.** These are **price zones** — not precise lines — where buying or selling pressure is expected to be strong enough to **reverse the direction of the trend** (S89). A *support* level is a price area where demand tends to emerge and halt a decline; a *resistance* level is where selling interest tends to emerge and halt a rise (S94). They are commonly explained by investor psychology and **loss aversion** (S89). Once a support is decisively broken it often becomes resistance (and vice versa) — "role reversal" (S94).

**Momentum.** Two related but distinct ideas:
- *Cross-sectional momentum* (Jegadeesh & Titman 1993): past **winners** (best returns over the last 3–12 months) tend to keep outperforming past **losers** over the next 3–12 months (S92). This is the "relative strength" of technical analysts, now absorbed into standard finance as the momentum anomaly (S89).
- *Time-series momentum* (Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen 2012): a security's **own** past return predicts its future return — e.g., the past 12-month excess return is a positive predictor of the next return; it persists about a year then partially reverses (S91). This is pure trend-following on a single asset, distinct from the cross-sectional sort.

## Math / formulas
**Simple moving average (SMA).**
$$\text{SMA}_t(n)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}P_{t-i}$$

**Exponential moving average (EMA).**
$$\text{EMA}_t=\alpha P_t+(1-\alpha)\,\text{EMA}_{t-1},\qquad \alpha=\frac{2}{n+1}$$

**Golden / death cross.** A *golden cross* is when a shorter SMA crosses **above** a longer SMA (classically the 50-day above the 200-day); a *death cross* is the opposite. These are trend-continuation signals generated only after a trend is underway (S89, S95).

**Cross-sectional (12-1) momentum.** Rank stocks by past return over $[t-k,\,t-1]$ (the most recent month is typically **skipped** to avoid short-term reversal noise), then hold a long-winners / short-losers portfolio for $h$ months. The canonical specification is $k=12$, skip 1 month, hold 3–6 months (S92).

**Time-series momentum signal.** Position in asset $i$ at $t$ is $\text{sign}(r_{i,t-12,\,t-1})$, i.e., long if the past 12-month excess return is positive (S91).

**Support/resistance as reference levels.** No single formula; practitioners derive them from prior highs/lows, round numbers, moving averages, or volatility-based methods (e.g., VBSR in S89). The conceptual rule is: a level that repeatedly rejected price becomes a candidate future barrier; on breach, role reversal applies.

## Worked example / code
The snippet below is **pure stdlib** (deterministic, seeded) so it runs anywhere and demonstrates the mechanics: SMA crossover (golden/death cross) and a 12-1 momentum read. It uses **synthetic** data — replace `prices` with real daily closes for production use.

```python
import random

# Deterministic synthetic daily close (seeded). NOT real market data.
# For real data, load a close series, e.g. with pandas/yfinance (pinned below).
random.seed(42)
n = 504
prices = [100.0]
for _ in range(1, n):
    drift = 0.0003            # mild uptrend
    shock = random.gauss(0, 0.012)
    prices.append(prices[-1] * (1 + drift + shock))

def sma(x, w, t):
    if t + 1 < w:
        return None
    return sum(x[t - w + 1: t + 1]) / w

# Golden/death cross: SMA20 vs SMA50 (stand-ins for 50/200 on a short sample)
short_w, long_w = 20, 50
prev = None
crosses = []
state = None
for t in range(long_w, n):
    s, l = sma(prices, short_w, t), sma(prices, long_w, t)
    state = "long" if s > l else "flat"
    if prev is not None and state != prev:
        crosses.append((t, state))
    prev = state

# 12-1 momentum (skip most recent month ~21d) at final bar
m, skip = 252, 21
last = prices[-1]
base = prices[-(m + skip)]
mom12_1 = last / base - 1
print(f"final close: {last:.2f}")
print(f"golden/death crosses (SMA{short_w}/{long_w}): {len(crosses)} -> {crosses[:3]} ...")
print(f"12-1 momentum (skip 1m) final: {mom12_1:+.2%}")
print(f"trend state at end: {state}")

# Pinned real-data variant (verify environment separately):
#   pip install "pandas==2.2.2" "yfinance==0.2.40"
#   import pandas as pd, yfinance as yf
#   px = yf.download("SPY", start="2018-01-01", auto_adjust=True)["Close"]
#   sma50 = px.rolling(50).mean(); sma200 = px.rolling(200).mean()
#   signal = (sma50 > sma200).astype(int).diff().fillna(0)
```
Output on this seeded series: `final close: 141.31`, `golden/death crosses (SMA20/50): 6`, `12-1 momentum (skip 1m) final: -5.56%`, `trend state at end: flat`. **Data source note:** the synthetic path is for mechanics only; production work must use real, **point-in-time, survivorship-free** closes (see KB 13-data-and-tooling, 15-pitfalls-and-antipatterns/survivorship).

## Assumptions & limitations
- **"History repeats" is an assumption, not a law.** TA breaks down precisely in regime shifts, structural breaks, and when a rule becomes so popular it is arbitraged away (S89's Adaptive Market Hypothesis framing).
- **Indicators are reactive/lagging.** MA crossovers fire only *after* a trend is established; the CFA monograph explicitly notes moving averages suffer "high trading costs, delayed signals, and short-lived erroneous trading signals no matter how the average is calculated" (S89).
- **Support/resistance are zones, not lines.** Breakouts can be false (bull/bear traps); volume confirmation is often required and is itself imperfect.
- **Costs dominate churned strategies.** TA strategies trade frequently; ignoring commissions, spread, slippage, and market impact can turn a gross edge negative (see KB 08-backtesting-methodology).
- **Backtests are prone to data-mining bias.** Brock et al. tested **7,846** variants of two rules; selecting the best ex post is not evidence of future edge (S89; see KB 05-stats-and-ml, 15-pitfalls-and-antipatterns/data-snooping-phacking).

## Empirical evidence
*(Three-bucket: **robust** = momentum premium; **emerging** = simple MA-rule profitability in current US equities; **folklore** = single-pattern/indicator clairvoyance.)*

- **Brock, Lakonishok & LeBaron (1992)** on the Dow Jones Index 1897–1986: a variable-length MA produced buy-period average daily returns of **+0.042% (~+12%/yr)** and sell-period **−0.025% (~−7%/yr)**; bootstrap rejected random-walk/AR(1)/GARCH-M nulls. *But* 7,846 parameter combinations were tried → data-mining caveat. Sullivan, Timmermann & White (1999) reviewed it and noted historical superiority "may have disappeared" as markets became more efficient. **Fang, Jacobsen & Qin (2014)**, using the *exact* Brock rules on a true out-of-sample 1987–2011 test, found **no evidence** the rules predict — concluding the earlier result was "probably a result of bias" rather than a real, persistent edge (S89, S90).
- **Jegadeesh & Titman (1993)**, 1965–1989: the 6-month-lookback / 6-month-hold relative-strength strategy earned a **compounded 12.01% per year** abnormal return; not explained by systematic risk; consistent with delayed price reaction to firm-specific information (S92).
- **Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen (2012)**: time-series momentum was positive in **58 of 58** liquid futures contracts across equities, currencies, commodities, and bonds; persists ~1 year then partially reverses; a diversified cross-asset portfolio delivered abnormal returns with little standard-factor exposure and performed best in extremes (S91).
- **Park & Irwin (2007)** meta-review of 92 modern studies: **58** found profitability, **24** negative, **10** mixed; technical strategies were economically profitable in US stocks **until the late 1980s but not thereafter**, while FX and emerging markets remained more favorable (S93).
- **Cowles (1933/34)** found simple trend-following underperformed buy-and-hold; **Brown, Goetzmann & Kumar (1998)** reconsidered and reported a Dow-theory portfolio with Sharpe **0.559 vs 0.456** (buy-and-hold) and a positive Jensen alpha of **4.04%** (S89).

## Conflicting views
- **EMH vs TA.** Malkiel concluded that under scientific scrutiny "chart-reading must share a pedestal with alchemy," noting random coin-toss charts look like real ones (S89). *Counter:* Hasanhodzic, Lo & Viola (2010) showed subjects could distinguish actual from randomized return series (p ≤ 0.5%) when given feedback — implying charts can carry information for skilled analysts (S89).
- **Academic vs practitioner.** Many simple rules fail OOS (Fang 2014; Park & Irwin 2007), yet practitioners report value via **fusion** (momentum combined with value; Asness 1997, 2013, 2015 in S89). The reconciliation: standalone simple rules are weak; integrated, cost-aware systems can add value.
- **Has momentum "faded"?** Bhattacharya, Kumar & Sonaer (2012) find US momentum profits became insignificant after the late 1990s; Asness, Moskowitz & Pedersen (2013, "Value and Momentum Everywhere") find momentum robust across global asset classes. Lo's **Adaptive Market Hypothesis** frames this as techniques working at some times/markets and not others as traders adapt (S89).
- **Pattern analysis.** FRBNY (1995) found head-and-shoulders had *some* predictive power in FX (DM, yen) but not other currencies; Bulkowski (2005) reached similar mixed conclusions — patterns are occasionally useful, not universally reliable (S89).

## Common mistakes
- **Data mining / overfitting.** Searching 1–10,000-day MAs (or stacking indicators) and publishing the best fit is the classic data-mining bias; require out-of-sample tests, deflated Sharpe ratios, and data-snooping corrections (KB 05, 08, 15).
- **Ignoring transaction costs and slippage.** TA churns; net-of-cost edge is what matters (KB 08).
- **Look-ahead bias.** Using adjusted (split/dividend) prices as if unadjusted, or signals computed with future information, inflates results (KB 05, 15).
- **Survivorship bias.** Selecting today's index members and backtesting TA on them ignores delisted firms (KB 15-pitfalls/survivorship).
- **Treating support/resistance as exact levels** and neglecting false breakouts / volume confirmation.
- **Believing "the chart predicts"** rather than "the chart summarizes probabilities under a repeating-psychology assumption."
